Spain Cyber Threat Landscape Report – April 2025

This report outlines key cyber threats facing Spain in 2025, with data-driven insights into ransomware, phishing, critical infrastructure risks, and national cybersecurity measures.

Executive Summary

Spain's cyber threat landscape in 2025 is marked by an increase in ransomware attacks, sophisticated phishing campaigns, and risks to critical infrastructure. Government and private sectors alike are targets, despite ongoing efforts to enhance national cybersecurity posture.

Key Threat Vectors

Ransomware Attacks

  • 72% increase in ransomware incidents in 2024.
  • Top groups: LockBit (19), RansomHub (9), Cactus (5).
  • Most targeted sectors: Manufacturing (20%), Tech (7%), Real Estate (7%), Healthcare (7%).

Phishing and Credential Theft

  • Banking sector: 47.47% of phishing incidents.
  • Finance and telecom sectors also highly affected.

Data Breaches and Dark Web Exposure

  • 130,000 healthcare records exposed on dark web.
  • Breaches also reported in government and sports sectors.

DDoS Attacks

  • Over 74,000 DDoS attacks recorded in 2024.
  • Peak bandwidth reached 499.95 Gbps.

Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs)

  • Geopolitical tensions (e.g., Russia–Ukraine conflict) contributing to hybrid threats.
  • Growth of cybercrime-as-a-service model facilitating access to sophisticated attack tools.

Governmental and Regulatory Response

  • Draft Law on Cybersecurity Coordination and Governance introduced in 2025.
  • €1 billion investment into digital skills and cybersecurity infrastructure.
  • Goal: Add 20,000 cybersecurity, AI, and data specialists by 2026.

Sector-Specific Threats

Manufacturing

Most targeted sector for ransomware attacks; operational disruptions are a key risk.

Finance

Heavy phishing activity; must meet new EU compliance standards like DORA.

Healthcare

Major data leaks reported; patient data security remains a pressing issue.

Energy

Cyberattacks targeting energy infrastructure (e.g., Iberdrola breach) highlight vulnerabilities.

Strategic Recommendations

  • Enhance detection and response for ransomware and phishing attacks.
  • Prioritize regulatory compliance with EU laws (e.g., DORA, NIS2).
  • Boost public-private threat intel collaboration.
  • Close the workforce gap through targeted cybersecurity education.

Conclusion

Cyber threats in Spain are growing in volume and sophistication. A proactive, intelligence-driven, and collaborative approach is essential to defend against evolving threats targeting both government and private infrastructure in 2025 and beyond.
